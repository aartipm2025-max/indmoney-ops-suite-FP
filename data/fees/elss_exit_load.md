# ELSS Exit Load — Fee Scenario Explainer
> ≤6 bullet structured explainer. Facts-only. Last checked: 2026-04-23.

## What it is
Exit load is a fee charged by mutual funds when investors redeem units before a specified holding period. It is expressed as a percentage of the redemption value and reduces the final payout received by the investor. The purpose of exit load is to discourage short-term trading and ensure investors stay invested for the intended horizon.

## Who's involved
Investor, AMC (SBI Mutual Fund), SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India — regulator).

## How it's calculated
- Exit load is typically a fixed percentage of the NAV at which units are redeemed.
- Different mutual funds have different exit load structures depending on the scheme type and holding period.
- In SBI ELSS Tax Saver Fund, exit load is NIL.
- However, investments are subject to a mandatory 3-year lock-in period that prevents redemption during that period entirely.
- Because the lock-in period exceeds 1 year, STCG does not apply to ELSS; only LTCG rules apply on redemption after the lock-in.

## Worked Example
Investor invests ₹1,50,000 in SBI ELSS Tax Saver Fund on 1 April 2023. The investor claims ₹1,50,000 deduction under Section 80C for FY 2023-24. On 2 April 2026 (after mandatory 3-year lock-in), the investment value is ₹2,10,000. Gain = ₹60,000. Since LTCG up to ₹1,25,000 per FY is exempt (as per 2025-26 rules), LTCG tax = ₹0. Exit load charged = ₹0 (NIL for ELSS).

## Edge cases and common misconceptions
- Can you redeem ELSS before 3 years? No. The units cannot be redeemed during the lock-in period — it is not just a fee, it is a restriction.
- What about SIP installments? Each SIP installment has its OWN 3-year lock-in measured from that installment's purchase date.
- Is exit load the same as lock-in? No. Exit load is a fee; lock-in is a redemption restriction. ELSS has lock-in but no exit load.
